Mug Shop

2020 - STUDENT PROJECT - 4 WEEKS

Mug Shop is a fictional e-commerce mobile application that specializes in coffee and brewing supplies.

Like most people who are firing on all caffeinated cylindars, Mug Shop is bold and straight to the point with it’s clear layout and personalized recommendations.

ROLE

  • UX Design
  • Wireframing
  • Art Director
  • UI Design

PROBLEM

MugShop needed a mobile application that provided a streamlined buying experience for the user. I needed to create as few screens as possible to get the userfromthehomescreentothe checkout fast.

OBJECTIVE

  • Design a mobile application for people who want to shop for key items without going into a physical store.
  • Create a company and establish it’s visual brand with a logo, colour pallete, and style guide for the mobile app to adhere to.

Research

Project research was provided. The key takeaway was that users wanted a quick and efficient way to shop for items. I decided to focus on flows that lowered the barrier of entry, encouraged buying at the users own pace, and having quick access to sales and promotions. 

Ideation and Wireframes

After creating the user flows, I started doing some rough sketches of each of the screens I would need. Once I was happy with the basic layout, I created some low fidelity wireframes that could be used in a prototype and tested before moving on.  

User Testing and Feedback

I asked a group of users to perform specific tasks in the prototype so I could collect feedback on how they were navigating it. The findings helped inform what the final design would look like.   

Design

I wanted something that communicated the boldness of coffee. I leaned into the palette of browns and creams using black as a neutral shade for things like icons and orange as a highlight to draw the users attention when necessary.  

Mug Shop

This project started off a lot more ‘bold’ in appearance than it ended up being, which is probably for the best. I originally took the idea of a cup of coffee further than maybe I should have, imagining it as if the Impact typeface was in charge of the style guide. Including lots of thick black borders around items and lines to separate content ended up creating a design that felt suffocating and kinda intimidating. In the final screens shown off here, I toned a lot of that back which has given it a much more approachable look. I think I could do more here by using product photos without a background, adding even more white space.

I’ve also come away with a better appreciation of button placement given the feedback I saw around my ‘Add to Wishlist’ button. I’m ultimately glad I was able to make it work without having to spell it out for the user with a verbal button label.
