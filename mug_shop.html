Mug Shop

2020 - STUDENT PROJECT - 4 WEEKS

Mug Shop is a fictional e-commerce mobile application that specializes in coffee and brewing supplies.

Like most people who are firing on all caffeinated cylindars, Mug Shop is bold and straight to the point with it’s clear layout and personalized recommendations.

Problem

Mug Shop needed a mobile application that provided a streamlined buying experience for the user. There had to be as few screens as possible between the home and checkout page.

Objective

Design a mobile application for people who want to shop for key items without going into a physical store.

Establish it’s visual brand with a logo, colour palette, and style guide

Role

UX Design
Wireframing
Art Director
UI Design

Research

The research for this project was provided ahead of time. The key takeaway was that users wanted a quick and efficient way to shop for items. I decided to focus on flows that lowered the barrier of entry, encouraged buying at the users own pace, and having quick access to sales and promotions.

Ideation and Wireframes

After creating the user flows, I started doing some rough sketches of each of the screens I would need. I wanted to keep the interface clean and accessible so I started with fairly large sized items. Once I was happy with the basic layout, I created some low fidelity wireframes that could be used in a prototype and tested before moving on.

User Testing and Feedback

I asked a group of users to perform specific tasks in the prototype so I could collect feedback on how they were navigating it. I wanted to ensure that the user flows could be completed easily with the provided screens. The findings helped inform what the final design would look like.

Users couldn’t find the ‘Add to Wishlist’ button and had difficulties navigating the app because of the verbiage being used in the menu bar.

Iteration

Based on the feedback I was given, I revisted certain areas of the design and worked to improve the usability.

Getting the Wishlist button right was key the targeted user flow and made me think about button placement more. Moving it from the top right corner of the screen down and intersecting with the product card helped draw the users attention to it more.

Designing the Brand

I wanted something that communicated the boldness of coffee. I leaned into the palette of browns and creams using black as a bold statement for things like item cards, icons and menu bars. Orange is used as a highlight to draw the users attention when necessary. It was inspired by the orange power light that was on my grandmothers coffee machine.

Nailing the Look

I reduced the useage of thick black lines throughout the design. I was relying on it too much to divide content when I could've been spacing elements better to achieve the same thing. This allowed the design to breath more allowing the typography and imagery to convey the boldness I was going for.


Decluttering the Bag Screen by moving the ‘delete’ function to a hidden slide over menu made it harder to accidentally delete items and the Bag Screen less overwhelming.

Reevaluating the visual hierarchy of the ‘Buy Now’ and ‘Add to Bag’ buttons, there was an opportunity to encourage users to purchase the item immediately instead of adding it to the bag, where it could be saved for later and possibly forgotten about.

The new emphasis on the ‘Buy Now’ button would hopefully generate sales more often.

Summary

This project started off a lot more ‘bold’ in appearance than it ended up being, which is probably for the best. I originally took the idea of a cup of coffee further than maybe I should have, imagining it as if the Impact typeface was in charge of the style guide. Including lots of thick black borders around items and lines to separate content ended up creating a design that felt suffocating and kinda intimidating. In the final screens shown off here, I toned a lot of that back which has given it a much more approachable look. I think I could do more here by using product photos without a background, adding even more white space.

I’ve also come away with a better appreciation of button placement given the feedback I saw around my ‘Add to Wishlist’ button. I’m ultimately glad I was able to make it work without having to spell it out for the user with a verbal button label.
